Group actionsüîó

A useful way to think about groups is to think of their elements as encoding ‚Äúsymmetries‚Äù of a particular object. For a concrete example, consider the group (R,+,0)(\bb{R}, +, 0)(R,+,0) of real numbers under addition, and consider the unit circle1 sitting in C\bb{C}C. We say that R\bb{R}R acts on the circle by counterclockwise rotation; What this means is that to each element x:Rx : \bb{R}x:R, we assign a map C‚ÜíC\bb{C} \to \bb{C}C‚ÜíC in a way compatible with the group structure: Additive inverses ‚Äútranslate to‚Äù inverse maps, addition translates to function composition, and the additive identity is mapped to the identity function.
