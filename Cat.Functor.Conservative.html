We say a functor is conservative if it reflects isomorphisms. More concretely, if f:A‚ÜíBf : A \to Bf:A‚ÜíB is some morphism C\ca{C}C, and if F(f)F(f)F(f) is an iso in D\ca{D}D, then fff must have already been an iso in C\ca{C}C!

As a general fact, conservative functors reflect limits that they preserve (given those limits exist in the first place!).

The rough proof sketch is as follows: Let KKK be some cone in CCC such that F(K)F(K)F(K) is a limit in DDD, and LLL a limit in CCC of the same diagram. By the universal property of LLL, there exists a map Œ∑\etaŒ∑ from the apex of KKK to the apex of LLL in CCC. Furthermore, as F(K)F(K)F(K) is a limit in DDD, F(Œ∑)F(\eta)F(Œ∑) becomes an isomorphism in DDD. However, FFF is conservative, which implies that Œ∑\etaŒ∑ was an isomorphism in CCC all along! This means that KKK must be a limit in CCC as well (see apex-iso‚Üíis-limit).
