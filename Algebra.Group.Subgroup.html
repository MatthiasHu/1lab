Subgroups🔗

A subgroup mmm of a group GGG is a monomorphism H→mGH \xto{m} GHm​G, that is, an object of the poset of subobjects Sub⁡(G)\Sub(G)Sub(G). Since group homomorphisms are injective exactly when their underlying function is an embedding, we can alternatively describe this as a condition on a predicate G→PropG \to \propG→Prop.

Subgroup : Group ℓ → Type (lsuc ℓ)
Subgroup {ℓ = ℓ} G = Precategory.Ob (Subobj (Groups ℓ) G)

A proposition H:G→PropH : G \to \propH:G→Prop of a group (G,⋆)(G, \star)(G,⋆) represents a subgroup if it contains the group unit, is closed under multiplication, and is closed under inverses.

record represents-subgroup (G : Group ℓ) (H : ℙ (G .fst)) : Type ℓ where
  open Group-on (G .snd)

  field
    has-unit : unit ∈ H
    has-⋆    : ∀ {x y} → x ∈ H → y ∈ H → (x ⋆ y) ∈ H
    has-inv  : ∀ {x} → x ∈ H → x ⁻¹ ∈ H

If HHH represents a subgroup, then its total space ΣH\Sigma HΣH inherits a group structure from GGG, and the first projection ΣH→G\Sigma H \to GΣH→G is a group homormophism.

rep-subgroup→group-on
  : (H : ℙ (G .fst)) → represents-subgroup G H → Group-on (Σ[ x ∈ G .fst ] x ∈ H)
rep-subgroup→group-on {G = G} H sg =
  make-group
    (Σ-is-hlevel 2 has-is-set λ x → is-prop→is-set (H x .is-tr))
    (unit , has-unit)
    (λ { (x , xin) (y , yin) → x ⋆ y , has-⋆ xin yin} )
    (λ { (x , xin) → (x ⁻¹ , has-inv xin) })
    (λ x y z → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) (sym associative))
    (λ x → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) inversel)
    (λ x → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) inverser)
    (λ x → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) idl)
  where open Group-on (G .snd)
        open represents-subgroup sg

predicate→subgroup : (H : ℙ (G .fst)) → represents-subgroup G H → Subgroup G
predicate→subgroup {G = G} H p = cut map , ism where
  map : Groups.Hom (Σ (∣_∣ ⊙ H) , rep-subgroup→group-on H p) G
  map .fst = fst
  map .snd .Group-hom.pres-⋆ x y = refl

  ism : Groups.is-monic map
  ism g h p = Forget-is-faithful
    (embedding→monic (Subset-proj-embedding (is-tr ⊙ H)) (fst g) (fst h) (ap fst p))

Kernels and Images🔗

To a group homomorphism f:A→Bf : A \to Bf:A→B we can associate two canonical subgroups, one of AAA and one of BBB: fff’s image, written im⁡f\im fimf, is the subgroup of BBB “reachable by mapping through fff”, and fff’s kernel, written ker⁡f\ker fkerf, is the subgroup of AAA which fff sends to the unit.

The kernel can be cheapily described as a limit: It is the equaliser of fff and the zero morphism — which, recall, is the unique map A→BA \to BA→B which breaks down as A→0→BA \to 0 \to BA→0→B.

module _ {ℓ} where
  open Canonical-kernels (Groups ℓ) ∅ᴳ Groups-equalisers public

  Ker-subgroup : ∀ {A B : Group ℓ} → Groups.Hom A B → Subgroup A
  Ker-subgroup f = cut kernel , Groups.is-equaliser→is-monic _ has-is-kernel where
    open Kernel (Ker f)

Every group homomorphism f:A→Bf : A \to Bf:A→B has an image im⁡f\im fimf, defined by equipping its set-theoretic image with a group structure inherited from BBB. More concretely, we can describe the elements of im⁡f\im fimf as the “mere fibres” of fff: They consist of a point y:By : By:B, together with (the truncation of) a fibre of fff over yyy. We multiply xxx (in the fibre over aaa) with yyy (in the fibre over bbb), giving the element xyxyxy in the fibre over ababab.

For reasons that will become clear later, we denote the image of fff, when regarded as its own group, by A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f), and reserve the notation im⁡f\im fimf for that group regarded as a subgroup of BBB.

The construction of a group structure on A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) is unsurprising, so we leave it in this <details> tag for the curious reader.

When this monomorphism is taken as primary, we refer to A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) as im⁡f\im fimf.

The reason for denoting the set-theoretic image of f:A→Bf : A \to Bf:A→B (which is a subobject of BBB, equipped with BBB’s group operation) by A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) is the first isomorphism theorem (though we phrase it more categorically): The image of fff serves as a quotient for (the congruence generated by) ker⁡f\ker fkerf.

Note: In more classical texts, the first isomorphism theorem is phrased in terms of two pre-existing objects A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) (defined as the set of cosets of ker⁡(f)\ker(f)ker(f) regarded as a subgroup) and im⁡f\im fimf (defined as above). Here we have opted for a more categorical phrasing of that theorem: We know what the universal property of A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) is — namely that it is a specific colimit — so the specific construction used to implement it does not matter.

Representing kernels🔗

If an evil wizard kidnaps your significant others and demands that you find out whether a predicate P:G→PropP : G \to \propP:G→Prop is a kernel, how would you go about doing it? Well, I should point out that no matter how evil the wizard is, they are still human: The predicate PPP definitely represents a subgroup, in the sense introduced above — so there’s definitely a group homomorphism ΣG↪G\Sigma G \mono GΣG↪G. All we need to figure out is whether there exists a group HHH and a map f:G→Hf : G \to Hf:G→H, such that ΣG≅ker⁡f\Sigma G \cong \ker fΣG≅kerf as subgroups of GGG.

We begin by assuming that we have a kernel and investigating some properties that the fibres of its inclusion have. Of course, the fibre over 000 is inhabited, and they are closed under multiplication and inverses, though we shall not make note of that here).

module _ {ℓ} {A B : Group ℓ} (f : Groups.Hom A B) where private
  module Ker[f] = Kernel (Ker f)
  module f = Group-hom (f .snd)
  module A = Group-on (A .snd)
  module B = Group-on (B .snd)

  kerf : Ker[f].ker .fst → A .fst
  kerf = Ker[f].kernel .fst

  has-zero : fibre kerf A.unit
  has-zero = (A.unit , f.pres-id) , refl

  has-⋆ : ∀ {x y} → fibre kerf x → fibre kerf y → fibre kerf (x A.⋆ y)
  has-⋆ ((a , p) , q) ((b , r) , s) =
    (a A.⋆ b , f.pres-⋆ _ _ ·· ap₂ B._⋆_ p r ·· B.idl) ,
    ap₂ A._⋆_ q s

It turns out that ker⁡f\ker fkerf is also closed under conjugation by elements of the enveloping group, in that if f(x)=1f(x) = 1f(x)=1 (quickly switching to “multiplicative” notation for the unit), then f(yxy−1)f(yxy^{-1})f(yxy−1) must be 111 as well: for we have f(y)f(x)f(y−1)=f(y)1f(y−1)=f(yy−1)=f(1)=1f(y)f(x)f(y^{-1}) = f(y)1f(y^{-1}) = f(yy^{-1}) = f(1) = 1f(y)f(x)f(y−1)=f(y)1f(y−1)=f(yy−1)=f(1)=1.

  has-conjugate : ∀ {x y} → fibre kerf x → fibre kerf (y A.⋆ x A.⋆ y A.⁻¹)
  has-conjugate {x} {y} ((a , p) , q) = (_ , path) , refl where
    path =
      f .fst (y A.⋆ (x A.— y))             ≡⟨ ap (f .fst) A.associative ⟩≡
      f .fst ((y A.⋆ x) A.— y)             ≡⟨ f.pres-diff ⟩≡
      f .fst (y A.⋆ x) B.— f .fst y        ≡⟨ ap (B._⋆ _) (f.pres-⋆ y x) ⟩≡
      (f .fst y B.⋆ f .fst x) B.— f .fst y ≡⟨ ap (B._⋆ _) (ap (_ B.⋆_) (ap (f .fst) (sym q) ∙ p) ∙ B.idr) ⟩≡
      f .fst y B.— f .fst y                ≡˘⟨ f.pres-diff ⟩≡˘
      f .fst (y A.— y)                     ≡⟨ ap (f .fst) A.inverser ∙ f.pres-id ⟩≡
      B.unit                               ∎

It turns out that this last property is enough to pick out exactly the kernels amongst the representations of subgroups: If HHH is closed under conjugation, then HHH generates an equivalence relation on the set underlying GGG (namely, (x−y)∈H(x - y) \in H(x−y)∈H), and equip the quotient of this equivalence relation with a group structure. The kernel of the quotient map G→G/HG \to G/HG→G/H is then HHH. We call a predicate representing a kernel a normal subgroup, and we denote this in shorthand by H⊴GH \unlhd GH⊴G.

record normal-subgroup (G : Group ℓ) (H : ℙ (G .fst)) : Type ℓ where
  open Group-on (G .snd)
  field
    has-rep : represents-subgroup G H
    has-conjugate : ∀ {x y} → x ∈ H → (y ⋆ x ⋆ y ⁻¹) ∈ H

  has-conjugatel : ∀ {x y} → y ∈ H → ((x ⋆ y) ⋆ x ⁻¹) ∈ H
  has-conjugatel yin = subst (_∈ H) associative (has-conjugate yin)

  has-comm : ∀ {x y} → (x ⋆ y) ∈ H → (y ⋆ x) ∈ H
  has-comm {x = x} {y} ∈ = subst (_∈ H) p (has-conjugate ∈) where
    p = x ⁻¹ ⋆ (x ⋆ y) ⋆ x ⁻¹ ⁻¹ ≡⟨ ap₂ _⋆_ refl (sym associative) ∙ (λ i → x ⁻¹ ⋆ x ⋆ y ⋆ inv-inv {x = x} i) ⟩≡
        x ⁻¹ ⋆ x ⋆ y ⋆ x         ≡⟨ associative ⟩≡
        (x ⁻¹ ⋆ x) ⋆ y ⋆ x       ≡⟨ ap₂ _⋆_ inversel refl ∙ idl ⟩≡
        y ⋆ x                    ∎

  open represents-subgroup has-rep public

So, suppose we have a normal subgroup H⊴GH \unlhd GH⊴G. We define the underlying type of the quotient G/HG/HG/H to be the quotient of the relation R(x,y)=(x−y)∈HR(x, y) = (x - y) \in HR(x,y)=(x−y)∈H; It can be equipped with a group operation inherited from GGG, but this is incredibly tedious to do.

To prove that the group operation _⋆_ descends to the quotient, we prove that it takes related inputs to related outputs — a characterisation of binary operations on quotients we can invoke since the relation we’re quotienting by is reflexive. It suffices to show that (yw−zx)∈H(yw - zx) \in H(yw−zx)∈H whenever w−xw - xw−x and y−zy - zy−z are both in HHH, which is a tedious but straightforward calculation:

      module

          (w-x∈ : (w ⋆ inv x) ∈ H)
          (y-z∈ : (y ⋆ inv z) ∈ H) where abstract

          (has-comm rem₂)

To define inverses on the quotient, it suffices to show that whenever (x−y)∈H(x - y) \in H(x−y)∈H, we also have (x−1−y)∈H(x^{-1} - y) \in H(x−1−y)∈H.

    inverse : G/H → G/H

Even after this tedious algebra, it still remains to show that the operation is associative and has inverses. Fortunately, since equality in a group is a proposition, these follow from the group axioms on GGG rather directly:

Before we show that the kernel of the quotient map is isomorphic to the subgroup we started with (and indeed, that this isomorphism commutes with the respective, so that they determine the same subobject of GGG), we must show that the relation (x−y)∈H(x - y) \in H(x−y)∈H is an equivalence relation; We can then appeal to effectivity of quotients to conclude that, if inc(x)=inc(y)\id{inc}(x) = \id{inc}(y)inc(x)=inc(y), then (x−y)∈H(x - y) \in H(x−y)∈H.

  private

The two halves of the isomorphism are now very straightforward to define: If we have inc(x)=inc(0)\id{inc}(x) = \id{inc}(0)inc(x)=inc(0), then x−0∈Hx - 0 \in Hx−0∈H by effectivity, and x∈Hx \in Hx∈H by the group laws. Conversely, if x∈Hx \in Hx∈H, then x−0∈Hx - 0 \in Hx−0∈H, thus they are identified in the quotient. Thus, the predicate inc(x)=inc(0)\id{inc}(x) = \id{inc}(0)inc(x)=inc(0) recovers the subgroup HHH; And (the total space of) that predicate is exactly the kernel of inc\id{inc}inc!
